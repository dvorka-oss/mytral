Fitness, Fatigue & Performance — The Trend

This chart isolates the three Banister metrics so you can see how fast each changes. Fitness (green) changes slowly — its decay time constant τ1 is set to 42 days (long-term adaptation). Fatigue (red) is ~6× faster with τ2 = 7 days (short-term fatigue). Performance (blue dotted) is the difference: it drops when fatigue spikes and rises when you rest before a race.
(Note: the Morton et al. 1990 paper used τ1 = 45, τ2 = 15 as an illustrative example. These are individual-specific parameters — v2 will fit them to your own race history.)

About TRIMP

{% if ff.can("TRIMP_ROCKS") %}

TRIMP (Training Impulse) is a sophisticated training load analytics model used to quantify the physiological stress of a workout. MyTraL implements the Banister fitness-fatigue-performance model (Banister et al., 1975; Busso 1997, 2002) which models your body's response to training as a system of two first-order filters with different time constants.

The model tracks three primary metrics:

  • Fitness (green)
    • A 42-day exponentially weighted moving average of your daily TRIMP, representing long-term positive adaptation. Gains accumulate slowly and decay slowly.
  • Fatigue (red)
    • A 7-day exponentially weighted moving average of your daily TRIMP, representing short-term negative effect. Fatigue accumulates quickly and dissipates quickly.
  • Performance (blue dashed)
    • p(t) = k1·Fitness(t) − k2·Fatigue(t). Your predicted performance at any point in time. Positive = fresh, negative = tired.

The model also credits recovery days: when your daily TRIMP is below the recovery threshold (25), the model applies a negative training impulse that actively reduces fatigue (Busso 2002 refinement).

Legacy metrics (ATRIMP, CTRIMP, TRIMPB) are preserved as co-equal KPI cards for athletes familiar with the classic ATRIMP/CTRIMP/TRIMPB labels.

TRIMP (Training Impulse) is a sophisticated training load analytics model used to quantify the physiological stress of a workout. It is fundamentally heart-rate based, calculating cumulative effort by factoring in your heart rate reserve, duration, and the exponential intensity of the session. Its biggest strength is its ability to objectively balance long-term fitness gains against short-term fatigue, giving athletes a precise blueprint for peak performance and injury prevention.

In addition to daily TRIMP scores, the model and its chart also tracks three key metrics:

  • ATRIMP (Acute TRIMP)
    • A 7-day exponentially weighted moving average of your daily TRIMP scores, representing short-term training load and fatigue.
  • CTRIMP (Chronic TRIMP)
    • A 42-day exponentially weighted moving average of your daily TRIMP scores, representing long-term training load and fitness.
  • TRIMPB (TRIMP Balance)
    • The clean delta between your fitness and your fatigue, calculated as CTRIMP - ATRIMP, showing the balance between short-term load and long-term load, i.e. the delta between fitness and fatigue.
